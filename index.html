Field notes from a builder.

Twenty years of shipping AI products at scale — Group PM at Google, then Group PM at Meta, leading teams that put features in front of hundreds of millions of people. I left Meta nearly two years ago because what I'd lost track of was being close to the work.

So I'm building things again. Mostly by myself, mostly on evenings and weekends. The first thing I shipped was a complete management platform for my wife's Pilates studio — three weekends of work that replaced four hours a week of admin with twenty minutes. The system is specific to her studio in ways no commercial scheduler will ever care about, and that turned out to be the entire point.

That build became the proof case for Kneaded.ai, and the thesis under it: AI just made bespoke software cheap enough to ship for a single customer, and the small businesses that figure that out first will leave generic SaaS behind. The other half of what I write about is why "human in the loop" UX is usually rubber-stamping — and the version that actually works.

When you start running dozens of Claude Code sessions in parallel, the workflow itself becomes the design problem. I open-sourced Command Center for Claude because I needed it — a local kanban for every session running on your Mac. Two field notes go into the operational fixes that made parallel sessions work and the cyborg posture they grew out of. The newest one — The Spec Is The Hard Part — is from a workshop with seven NVIDIA PMs about what an AI-native product organization actually needs once code stops being the bottleneck.

I take on a few advisory roles at a time for early-stage AI startups. If something here resonated, my email is below. The bar isn't an impressive demo — it's a clear product spec for the right thing.
